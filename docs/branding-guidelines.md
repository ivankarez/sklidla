# Sklidla branding guidelines

## Current source of truth

Right now, the onboarding flow is the best example of Sklidla's intended voice.

That means this document is based primarily on the copy in:

- `app/onboarding/index.tsx`
- `app/onboarding/macro-setup.tsx`
- `app/onboarding/ai-setup.tsx`
- `src/components/macro-calculator.tsx`

Other parts of the app are not fully aligned yet. When there is a conflict, follow this document and treat onboarding as the current north star.

## Brand voice in one sentence

Sklidla should sound like a sharp, funny, slightly unhinged person who tells the truth quickly, respects the user's intelligence, and never turns basic nutrition copy into cringey gym-bro theater.

## What works in the onboarding copy

The onboarding flow works when it does these things:

- It is direct.
  - `"YOUR DATA. YOUR DEVICE."`
  - `"AI IS 100% OPTIONAL."`
- It explains the product in normal language.
  - `"WE'LL DO THE MATH YOU DON'T WANT TO."`
  - `"SET IT UP ANYTIME IN SETTINGS."`
- It has a point of view.
  - `"NO CREEPY CLOUDS. NO SUBSCRIPTIONS."`
- It uses humor as seasoning, not as a replacement for meaning.
  - `"THIS APP ACTUALLY FITS YOUR CHAOS."`

The best onboarding lines are memorable because they are clear first and funny second.

## The tone we want

Sklidla should feel:

- funny
- blunt
- confident
- human
- specific
- anti-corporate
- privacy-first

Sklidla should not feel:

- try-hard
- random
- chronically online
- mean for no reason
- like a fake drill sergeant
- like a supplements ad
- like a startup landing page written by ChatGPT in 2022

## Core writing principles

### 1. Clarity beats performance

The joke cannot do the job of the sentence.

If the user cannot immediately understand what happened, what to do next, or why something matters, the copy failed.

Write the meaning first. Then add attitude if it still reads naturally.

### 2. Sound like a person, not a catchphrase machine

Good Sklidla copy sounds like something a real person would actually say out loud.

Use contractions. Use everyday words. Use a rhythm that feels spoken, not fabricated.

Good:

- `"We'll do the math you don't want to."`
- `"Set it up anytime in Settings."`
- `"Your key, your cost control."`

Bad:

- `"Commence nutrition optimization."`
- `"Activate caloric intelligence."`
- `"Macronutrient dominance achieved."`

### 3. Be weird on purpose

The product can be loud and a little unhinged, but the weirdness has to be intentional.

One strong line is memorable. Five strong lines in a row is exhausting.

Use punchy moments to add flavor, then return to normal human language.

### 4. Respect the user's intelligence

Do not over-explain simple ideas. Do not use wellness fluff. Do not patronize the user.

Sklidla should feel like a smart tool with personality, not a motivational poster.

### 5. The experience should be fun, not forced

Funny copy should make the app feel distinct and enjoyable to use.

It should not feel like the app is constantly trying to prove how edgy it is.

If a line feels like it wants applause for being weird, rewrite it.

## Hard rules

### Do not call food "fuel"

Calling food `"fuel"` is not funny, not fresh, and not distinctive.

It makes the app sound like generic fitness marketing, which is exactly what Sklidla should avoid.

Say `"food"`, `"meal"`, `"what you ate"`, or another normal phrase instead.

Good:

- `"Log your meal."`
- `"Add what you ate."`
- `"Scan food label."`

Bad:

- `"Fuel acquired."`
- `"Log your fuel."`
- `"Feed the machine."`

### Do not confuse aggression with personality

Not every action needs to sound intense.

A few spiky lines can work. If every screen sounds like a shouting contest, the app stops being fun and starts being embarrassing.

### Do not hide basic meaning behind jokes

If the UI needs to say `"Save"`, `"Try again"`, `"Set macro goals"`, or `"Add food"`, say that clearly.

Personality can sit on top of the message, but it cannot replace the message.

## Practical voice rules

### Prefer this

- short sentences
- plain English
- dry humor
- specific wording
- confident statements
- real conversational rhythm

### Avoid this

- buzzwords
- biohacker jargon
- fake macho language
- forced slang
- motivational clichés
- repeated war, beast, grind, or hustle metaphors

## Copy pattern: clear first, punch second

This is the default Sklidla formula:

1. State the thing clearly.
2. Add one twist of personality.
3. Stop before it becomes a bit.

Examples:

- `"Your data stays on your device. Period."`
- `"We'll do the math you don't want to."`
- `"Snap your meal. Get macro estimates in seconds."`

## Onboarding lessons to keep

### Good patterns worth reusing

- Privacy claims are blunt and easy to understand.
- Product benefits are explained in plain language.
- The app sounds confident without sounding polished to death.
- Some lines are funny because they feel tossed off, not overworked.

### Patterns to use carefully

- All caps are a visual style, not the whole personality.
- Big dramatic phrases work better as occasional spikes than as the default mode.
- Gym language should be rare and controlled.

## Do and don't examples

| Situation | Do | Don't |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Logging food | `"Log your meal"` | `"Log your fuel"` |
| Success state | `"You did the thing."` | `"Macronutrient conquest complete."` |
| Macro setup | `"We'll do the math you don't want to."` | `"Initiate physique protocol."` |
| Privacy message | `"Your data stays on your device."` | `"Deploying sovereign data architecture."` |
| AI setup | `"Your key, your cost control."` | `"Unlock next-generation nutrition intelligence."` |

## Writing checklist

Before shipping any new copy, ask:

- Would a normal person actually say this?
- Is the meaning obvious on first read?
- Is the joke helping, or just making noise?
- Does this sound distinct without sounding desperate?
- Did we accidentally turn food into `"fuel"` or some other generic fitness cliché?

If the answer to any of those is no, rewrite it.

## Short version

Sklidla is not clinical, polished, or wholesome.

It is sharp, funny, and a little chaotic.

But the copy still needs to sound human.

Write like a real person with good timing. Keep the meaning obvious. Keep the jokes dry. Do not call food `"fuel"`.
